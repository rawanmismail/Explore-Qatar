Geography of Qatar

Discover the unique landscape of the Arabian Peninsula

Location & Size

Qatar is a peninsula located on the northeastern coast of the Arabian Peninsula, jutting into the Persian Gulf. The country covers approximately 11,581 square kilometers (4,471 square miles), making it one of the smallest countries in the Middle East. Despite its modest size, Qatar's strategic location has made it a significant player in regional and global affairs.

Landscape & Terrain

Qatar's landscape is predominantly flat and barren desert, characterized by sand dunes and rocky plains. The terrain gradually slopes from the center of the peninsula toward the coast. The country's highest point, Qurayn Abu al Bawl, reaches only 103 meters above sea level in the southern region.

The northern region features limestone outcrops and shallow depressions, while the southern areas are dominated by sand dunes. The eastern coast is marked by sabkhas (salt flats) and mangrove stands, particularly around Al Khor and Al Dhakira.

Despite the arid climate, Qatar has areas of natural vegetation including acacia trees, date palms, and desert shrubs, particularly in wadis and near water sources.

Borders & Neighboring Countries

Qatar shares its only land border with Saudi Arabia to the south, spanning approximately 87 kilometers. The rest of the country is surrounded by the Persian Gulf, with maritime boundaries near Bahrain to the northwest, the United Arab Emirates to the southeast, and Iran across the Gulf to the north.

Did You Know?

  • Qatar's name is believed to derive from "Qatara," meaning "to flow" or "to drip," possibly referring to the country's natural springs.
  • The Inland Sea (Khor Al Adaid) is one of the few places in the world where the sea meets the desert directly.
  • Qatar has no permanent rivers or lakes, relying on desalination plants and groundwater for its water supply.
  • The country's shape resembles a thumb extending into the Persian Gulf.
  • Qatar's strategic location made it an important pearling and trading center for centuries.

Natural Resources

Qatar's most significant natural resource is the North Field, the world's largest single non-associated natural gas field. This massive offshore gas field extends into Iranian waters (where it's known as South Pars) and contains approximately 10% of the world's known natural gas reserves.

The country also has substantial oil reserves, limestone deposits used in construction, and gypsum. Qatar's petroleum and natural gas resources have transformed it from a pearling and fishing economy into one of the world's wealthiest nations per capita.
